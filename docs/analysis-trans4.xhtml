230 Of all things with life and understanding,
231 we women are the most unfortunate.
232 First, we need a husband, someone we get
233 for an excessive price. He then becomes
234 the ruler of our bodies. And this misfortune
235 adds still more troubles to the grief we have.
236 Then comes the crucial struggle: this husband
237 we have selected, is he good or bad?
238 For a divorce loses women all respect,
239 yet we can’t refuse to take a husband.
240 Then, when she goes into her husband’s home,
241 with its new rules and different customs,
242 she needs a prophet’s skill to sort out the man
243 whose bed she shares. She can’t learn that at home.
244 Once we’ve worked hard at this, and with success,
245 our husband accepts the marriage yoke
246 and lives in peace—an enviable life.
247 But if the marriage doesn’t work, then death
248 is much to be preferred. When the man tires
249 of the company he keeps at home, he leaves,
250 seeking relief for his distress elsewhere,
251 outside the home. He gets his satisfaction
252 with some male friend or someone his own age.
253 We women have to look at just one man.
254 Men tell us we live safe and secure at home,
255 while they must go to battle with their spears.
256 How stupid they are! I’d rather stand there
257 three times in battle holding up my shield
258 than give birth once. But your story and mine
259 are not the same. For you have a city,
260 you have your father’s house, enjoy your life
261 with friends for company. But I’m alone.
262 I have no city, and I’m being abused
263 by my own husband. I was carried off,
264 a trophy from a barbarian land.
265 I have no mother, brother, or relation
266 to shelter with in this extremity.

Ian Johnston's translation of Medea by Euripidies streamlines the language of the text and make sit accessible to readers unfamilair with the classical texts and literature. According to Ian Johnston's bio, "He is known for his powerful and direct style, and his ability to make the works accessible to the modern reader while remaining faithful to the original work." This means the text has an element of simplicity potentially not avaliable in other texts, which may offer to change the reading. Johnston's translation of this passage guides the reader towards an angry Medea, one fed up with her patriarchal circumstances. Lines such as 256, "How stupid they are!" contribute to the passion in her emotion, her intolerance of her circumstances. This translation shows the character at a breaking point after being bruised by the men in her life, as is evident in lines 262 and 263, where she states that she's been abused. Johnston's Medea is one who clearly sees societies inequalities and isn't afraid to call them out. Johnston's translation background is one that stems from educating others. He taught in high schools and colleges in Canada, breaking down information to make it easier for others to understand.
