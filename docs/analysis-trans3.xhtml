Of all things that have life and sense we
women are the most hapless creatures; first
must we buy a husband at an exorbitant
price, and o’er ourselves a tyrant set which
is an evil worse than the first;
and herein lies the most important issue,
whether our choice be good or bad. For
divorce is discreditable to women, nor can
we disown our lords. Next must the wife,
coming as she does to ways and customs
new, since she hath not learnt the lesson in
her home, have a diviner’s eye
to see how best to treat the partner of her life. If
haply we perform these tasks with
thoroughness and tact, and the husband
live with us, without resenting the yoke, our
life is a happy one; if not, ’twere best to die.
But when a man is vexed with what he
finds indoors,
he goeth forth and rids his soul of its
disgust, betaking him to some friend or
comrade of like age; whilst we must needs
regard his single self.
And yet they say we live secure at home,
while they are at the wars,
with their sorry reasoning, for I would
gladly take my stand in battle array three
times o’er, than once give
birth.
But enough! this language suits not thee as
it does me; thou hast a city here, a father’s
house, some joy in life, and friends to share
thy thoughts,
but I am destitute, without a city, and
therefore scorned by my husband, a captive
I from a foreign shore, with no mother,
brother, or kinsman in whom to find a new
haven of refuge from this calamity.

Translation 3: Analysis

As the most modern example out of our translations, the language seems to reflect a diction similar to one that people use today. Nonethless, the antiquated theme of patriarchy is still prevalant throughout. Patriarchy shows up in these lines when Medea reflects on her status as a women. She recognizes that in a marriage she will likely lose all autonpmy, but divorce is extremely frowned up, especially for women. She explains how even though she has family and friends, she still feels like a captive in her marriage, as she has no autonpmy in the relationship. As a woman herself, Lisa Cerrato, the author of the translation, likely had some understanding of this notion. It is not unlikely that it influenced the way she translated the work, as she wanted the message to be unwaveringly clear. Cerrato works at Tufts University at the Perseus Digital Library, with a focus on user support and outreach. With this in mind, it is clear how Cerrato writes her translation with the goal of making it understandable to readers. Additionally, Cerrato's interests include furthering the study of classical Greek and Latin. Once again, a clear translation proves to be important to Cerrato. She likely took special care in the way she re-wrote the text, as she wants it to reach as many people as possible. The themes and messages must be clear in order to achieve her goal of spreading that knowledge.
