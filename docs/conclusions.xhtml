Final Conclusions

Overall Analysis of the Four Translations

From an analysis of the bar charts alongside general averages, it can be gleaned that patriarchy was by far the largest theme within the text. Across translations, mentions of patriarchy were at the forefront of the texts, followed closely by themes of family. While these are the largest sets of data, interpreter bias should be accounted for, as data set 4 contains a larger margin of data points compared to the prior three. Additionally, the translations themselves factor into the overall count: translation four was in simplified language, while translations 1-3 contained more advanced language. Despite these accounts of error, the overall analysis can still be viewed in agreement when seeing Medea as a victim of her patriarchal surroundings. There are 14 mentions of autonomy/issues relating to autonomy across the translations, which further pushes the idea that Medea is subject to the patriarchy around her, and is expressing her anger in the text. Our group’s markup demonstrates how Medea’s speech is a clear critique of patriarchy and its articulation of women’s intellectual and emotional depth. However, the play ultimately frames Medea’s response to Jason’s betrayal, especially the vengeful murder of her children, as both excessive and unforgivable. Women are portrayed as sympathetic yet dangerous, and predisposed to ‘wickedness’ or ‘cleverness.’ The chorus’s limited resistance to Medea’s wrath further normalizes her extreme nature, rather than critically challenging it. While our markups reveal how deeply the text engages with women’s oppression, through themes of autonomy, isolation, and inequality, it represents how the play itself exposes injustice but does not fully escape the misogynistic framework of its time. Medea is allowed to speak powerfully, but she is also a warning of the evils women are capable of ‘in nature.’
