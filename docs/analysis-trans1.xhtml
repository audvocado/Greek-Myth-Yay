273 Of all things upon earth that bleed and grow,
274 A herb most bruised is woman. We must pay
275 Our store of gold, hoarded for that one day,
276 To buy us some man's love; and lo, they bring
277 A master of our flesh! There comes the sting
278 Of the whole shame. And then the jeopardy,
279 For good or ill, what shall that master be;
280 Reject she cannot: and if he but stays
281 His suit, 'tis shame on all that woman's days.
282 =So thrown amid new laws, new places, why,
283 'Tis magic she must have, or prophecy—
284 Home never taught her that —how best to guide
285 Toward peace this thing that sleepeth at her side.
286 And she who, labouring long, shall find some way
287 Whereby her lord may bear with her, nor fray
288 His yoke too fiercely, blessed is the breath
289 That woman draws! Else, let her pray for death.
290 Her lord, if he be wearied of the face
291 Withindoors, gets him forth; some merrier place
292 Will ease his heart: but she waits on, her whole
293 Vision enchainèd on a single soul.
294 And then, forsooth, 'tis they that face the call
295 Of war, while we sit sheltered, hid from all
296 Peril!—False mocking! Sooner would I stand
297 Three times to face their battles, shield in hand,
298 Than bear one child.
